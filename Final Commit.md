For Campus Safety, the Clery Crime Report Falls Short

By Terrance Williams

Since 2022, Adam Lehavi’s Address Hate <https://antisemitism-demo-site.web.app/analytics> project has identified at least 51 incidents of antisemitism on the campus of American University. His database scrapes campus media, in this case The Eagle, AWOL, and BlackPrint, for reports of antisemitism and on-campus hate crimes. According to the university's Annual Security Report <https://www.american.edu/finance/publicsafety/upload/2025-annual-security-report.pdf>, however, there have only been 19. 

This isn't isolated just to hate crimes, and American is not an anomaly.  The nature of the discrepancies lie with the Clery Act, and how universities across the country address campus safety. <https://beckyyts.github.io/au-crime-analysis/au_campus_map.html>

Who was Jeanne Clery

Jeanne Clery was a student at Lehigh University in Pennsylvania. In 1986, she was raped and murdered in her college dorm. In the aftermath, it was discovered that the university had over 30 violent events in the three years prior, which they had not made public. Her death led to the passage of what would later be called The Clery Act. Under the provisions of the act, universities are required to publish an annual security report and daily crime logs. The act applies to all universities that receive federal funding.

Under the Clery Act, universities are required to report instances of criminal homicide, sexual assault, robbery, aggravated assault, burglary, motor vehicle theft, and arson. In 2024, hate crimes and hazing were added to the list of reportable crimes. The problem lies in how different universities are allowed to report crimes. 

Anastasios Kapentanakos is the Clery Crime Coordinator at American University. He said the annual report is mostly just statistical. “Schools are in effect just reporting enforcement of their code of conduct.” 

Take, for example, simple possession of marijuana. In the District, recreational marijuana was legalized for those over 21. However, since AU is a tobacco and drug-free campus, If a student under 18 is found with it, it would be a minor in possession, which is a misdemeanor under DC law. At the same time, officers have wide authority over the penalty. For students over 21, it would simply be a student code of conduct violation, which doesn’t have to be reported as a crime.

The same is true for alcohol. For more serious crimes, such as sexual harassment or assault, the numbers depend on a range of factors. At AU, for example, there were 14 reports to campus police for sexual offenses between 2022 and 2024 <https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1hEdu-ZRdJexftIxFJ818JTWQmufH5TuWhgkClD3SU9k/edit?gid=2103337021#gid=2103337021>. Six were designated Title IX cases, four were closed, one was closed by arrest, one was referred to MPD, one was still labeled pending, and one was found to be unfounded. The Annual Security Report lists 10 for that same time period.

What gets left out

Currently, only the seven Clery Act crimes are required to be reported by universities. Hazing and hate crimes were added in 2024, and the first report with those numbers included won't be available until 2026. Here, universities are allowed discretion as well. 


“A Clery hate crime might fall under ‘harassment’, or ‘threat to do bodily harm’ and include certain language,” said Kapetanakos. “Hate crime in criminal law is not an actual thing.” That creates a problem, especially when antisemitism and hate crimes are on the rise. 

“One of the biggest things is hate crimes are under-reported in all demographics,’ Lehavi says. His database backs it up. While those numbers are not required until next year, AU’s report includes them already. However, the 19 reported over the last three years is likely an undercount.


Kapentanakos said this, along with differing definitions between campus policy and DC codes, makes it likely that these issues persist across campuses. If a university doesn't adhere to the letter of the law, however, it can be fined over $70,000 per offense by the Department of Education. 

Whats next

For now, the Clery Report remains the main reporting tool used by campuses to track and report crime trends. Without better comparison tools, it alone paints a skewed picture of what is really happening on America’s college campuses. With the help of people like Adam, a way to create a more accurate picture may be on the horizon. 

“Without proper data, there is nothing to take away,” he said. “There is a strong collaboration that needs to happen between the data scientists and the social scientists.” His database is one of the few, if not the only one, of its kind. That alone isn’t enough.

Kapentanakos says there needs to be clearer-cut directives from the Department of Education. “They say, ‘the way you cover this is up to you, but we won’t tell you how to cover it.” For his part, he said he tends to overreport to ensure compliance and accuracy. Still, the information provided is murky at best.
