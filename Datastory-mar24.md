The data in this article was first referenced by Michelle Singletary in an article for the Washington Post, 'The enduring truth of Tupac's 'money for wars, but can't feed the poor'.
In her article, Singletary discusses the cost of the ongoing War with Iran and the price of munitions and missles in relation to SNAP benefits, increases in gas prices, and the rising cost of 
healthcare. Here, she references the data contained in the gallup polls. 
In one poll of over 20,000 Americans over the course of six months, Gallup estimated from their representative sample that around 1.6 million Americans who make over $240K per year were being forced to make
financial trade-offs to account for the rising cost of medical bills. From their aggregate data, they estimated that around 33% of American adults have stretched a current prescription, borrowed money, skipped a meal, drove less, 
or cut back on utilities in order to pay for healthcare or medicine. These numbers increase when considering individuals who don't have insurance vs. those who do, and the number rises as high as
55% with individuals with a household income of less than $24K. 

The data from Gallup is presented in 7 different charts which are all straightforward and clear, with a good use of color, and text. There is at least one poll which used a different dataset, which is mentioned in 
the text of the article but not noted in the graph itself, other than having different dates of data collection. It would be helpful for it to be more obvious that an entirely different survey was used to collect this data.

The most interesting part of this data is that it included three categories of individuals with incomes above $120K, which I feel isn't done in a lot of surveys that display this kind of data.
